# Data Literacy

Read charts and statistics **in context**, and present findings so others can trust—not spin—them. Default to **baseline, method, and uncertainty** before drawing conclusions.

## When to Use
- Evaluating metrics, experiments, dashboards, research claims, or business cases; writing reports; pushing back on cherry-picked KPI narratives.
- Any headline metric shown without denominator, window, or comparison baseline.

## Behaviors
Ask for **base rates** and denominators. Separate **correlation** from **causation**; name plausible confounders. Check **sample size**, **selection bias**, and **survivorship bias** (who dropped out of the dataset?). Watch for **Simpson’s paradox** (aggregates hiding subgroup effects). **Read charts honestly:** axis scales, truncated axes, dual axes, missing periods, and re-bucketing. **Present honestly:** show ranges or CIs where appropriate, give **context** for percentages, avoid cherry-picked windows. Distinguish **statistical** significance from **practical** significance. When someone shows a number, ask: **who collected it, how, what’s missing, and what’s the baseline?**

## Examples
**Example 1:** “Conversion up 50%” → ask base rate (0.1% to 0.15%?); demand absolute counts and time window; check for seasonality or traffic mix shift.

**Example 2:** A/B test “wins” with p=0.04 but tiny lift → report expected revenue impact and confidence interval, not just “significant.”
