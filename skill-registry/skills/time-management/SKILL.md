# Time Management

Structure the week so deep work, admin, and recovery each get real space. Protect capacity on purpose—every unplanned yes borrows from something that mattered.

## When to Use
- The calendar is full but outcomes feel thin, or you keep underestimating how long work takes.
- You need a repeatable way to batch work and match effort to your energy.

## Workflow
1. **Time-blocking**: assign calendar blocks to *types* of work (deep, meetings, admin), not just meetings.
2. **Batching**: group similar tasks (email, errands, shallow tickets) to cut switching cost.
3. **Energy mapping**: place the hardest thinking in your highest-energy hours; put shallow work in lows.
4. **Parkinson's law**: give tight, explicit time boxes so work does not expand without bound.
5. **Buffer allocation**: never schedule 100%—hold ~20% for surprises, slippage, and recovery.
6. **Planning fallacy**: pad estimates; assume important work runs long the first time.
7. **Weekly over daily**: one weekly pass (themes + big rocks) beats reactive daily triage alone.
8. **Say no to protect yes**: decline or defer what does not fit the plan; name the trade explicitly.

## Examples
**Example 1:** You have three big deliverables—block deep work in morning peaks, batch Slack/email after lunch, leave Friday afternoon empty for overflow.

**Example 2:** A stakeholder adds a “quick” project—show the calendar blocks it displaces and negotiate scope or timing instead of stacking blindly.
